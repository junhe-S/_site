Working with Stock Returns

Jun He May 31, 2026

Returns, not prices, are the natural unit of analysis: they are roughly comparable across stocks and over time, and they are what asset-pricing theory speaks about.

Simple vs. log returns

A simple return over one period is the price change plus any dividend, divided by the starting price. A log return is the natural log of the gross return. Log returns add cleanly across time — the log return over a year is the sum of the monthly log returns — which makes them convenient for compounding and modelling. Simple returns aggregate cleanly across assets in a portfolio. Knowing which property you need tells you which to use.

Building a return series

From a panel of prices, sort within each stock by date, compute period-over-period returns, and be careful at the edges: the first observation per stock has no prior price, and gaps (missing days, delistings) must not be silently treated as zero returns.

Stylized facts

Daily return distributions are not normal. Two robust regularities stand out: fat tails — extreme moves happen far more often than a normal distribution predicts — and volatility clustering — large moves tend to follow large moves, so turbulent and calm periods come in stretches. Simple summary statistics and a few plots (histograms, rolling volatility) make these visible and motivate much of the modelling that follows.
