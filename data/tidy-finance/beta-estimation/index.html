Beta Estimation

Jun He May 31, 2026

Estimating market betas for thousands of stocks over decades is the first genuinely large-scale computation in the empirical workflow, and a good test of doing rolling calculations efficiently.

The regression

A stock's beta is the slope from regressing its excess return on the market's excess return over an estimation window. Conceptually simple; the work is in doing it repeatedly — one regression per stock per month, on a rolling window of past returns.

Rolling windows and frequency

Two choices shape the estimates. Window length trades off responsiveness against precision: a short window adapts to changing risk but is noisy; a long window is stable but stale. Data frequency matters too — daily data gives many observations per window but introduces microstructure noise, while monthly data is cleaner but sparser. Comparing daily- and monthly-based betas shows how much these choices move the answer.

Doing it at scale

The computational lesson is to structure the rolling regression so it runs efficiently across a large panel — grouping by stock, applying the window calculation in a vectorized way rather than looping naively — so that the full cross-section estimates in reasonable time.
