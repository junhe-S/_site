Difference-in-Differences

Jun He May 31, 2026

Difference-in-differences (DiD) is the most widely used quasi-experimental design in empirical finance: it estimates a treatment effect by comparing how outcomes change for a treated group versus a control group around an event.

The idea

Take the change in the outcome for the treated group before vs. after the event, and subtract the change over the same window for an untreated control group. The second difference nets out any trend common to both groups, isolating the part of the change attributable to the treatment.

The key assumption

DiD rests on parallel trends: absent the treatment, the two groups would have evolved similarly. This is not testable directly, but its plausibility is supported by checking that the groups moved together before the event — a pre-trend analysis. A clean pre-period and a sharp break at the event are what make the design credible.

Implementation and cautions

In practice DiD is run as a two-way fixed-effects regression with an interaction of treatment and post-period indicators, standard errors clustered at the unit level. Recent econometrics has flagged pitfalls when treatment is staggered across units at different times — the simple two-way specification can mislead — so staggered designs call for the newer estimators built to handle them.
