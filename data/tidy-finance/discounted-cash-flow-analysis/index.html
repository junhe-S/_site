Discounted Cash Flow Analysis

Jun He May 31, 2026

A discounted cash flow (DCF) valuation estimates what an asset is worth from first principles: the present value of the cash it will generate.

The mechanics

Three ingredients: a forecast of future free cash flows, a discount rate that reflects their risk and the time value of money, and a terminal value capturing everything beyond the explicit forecast horizon. Discounting each future cash flow back to today and summing gives the intrinsic value estimate.

The discount rate

The discount rate is usually a weighted average cost of capital — blending the cost of equity (often from a CAPM-style estimate) and the after-tax cost of debt by their weights in the capital structure. Small changes here move the valuation a lot, which is why DCF outputs should always be stress-tested.

Sensitivity

Because the terminal value often dominates and the inputs are uncertain, a DCF is best read as a range, not a point. Varying the growth rate and discount rate over plausible bounds shows how much of the answer is assumption versus signal — a healthy corrective to false precision.
