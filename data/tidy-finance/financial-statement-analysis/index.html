Financial Statement Analysis

Jun He May 31, 2026

Accounting data describes the firm behind the stock. Turning raw statement items into comparable ratios is what lets you compare firms of very different sizes and industries.

The statements

Three statements carry the information: the balance sheet (a snapshot of assets, liabilities, and equity), the income statement (revenues and expenses over a period), and the cash flow statement (where cash actually moved). Empirical work draws on items from all three.

Ratios

Raw dollar figures are not comparable across firms, so we scale them. Profitability ratios (e.g. return on assets, operating margin), leverage ratios (debt to assets), and valuation ratios (book-to-market, earnings yield) each normalize a statement item by another, producing measures that travel across the cross-section.

Why it matters here

These ratios become the firm characteristics that the asset-pricing chapters sort and regress on. Book-to-market, profitability, and investment, for instance, are the building blocks of the factor models later in the book — so careful, consistent construction here pays off downstream.
