Constrained Optimization and Backtesting

Jun He May 31, 2026

A portfolio that looks optimal on paper can fall apart once real-world frictions enter. This chapter adds constraints and transaction costs to the optimization, then backtests honestly to see what survives.

Realistic constraints

Unconstrained mean–variance solutions often demand large, unstable, heavily leveraged positions. Adding constraints — no short-selling, position limits, full investment — keeps weights sensible and makes the strategy implementable. These turn the optimization into a constrained quadratic program rather than a closed-form solution.

Transaction costs

Rebalancing is not free. Charging a cost proportional to turnover penalizes strategies that churn the portfolio, and changes the optimal weights: the optimizer trades off the benefit of moving toward the target against the cost of getting there. Ignoring costs is one of the most common ways a backtest overstates performance.

Backtesting honestly

The test is out-of-sample: at each rebalance date use only information available then, form the portfolio, and roll forward. Reporting net-of-cost returns, turnover, and drawdowns — not just gross Sharpe ratios — gives a truthful picture. The recurring lesson is that frictions and look-ahead bias separate a strategy that works on a slide from one that works in practice.
