Capital Asset Pricing Model

Jun He May 31, 2026

The CAPM is the first equilibrium theory of expected returns: in a world where everyone holds mean–variance efficient portfolios, only co-movement with the market is rewarded.

Beta

A stock's beta measures how its excess return moves with the market's excess return — formally, the covariance of the two divided by the market's variance. Empirically it is the slope from regressing the stock's excess return on the market's excess return. Beta above one means the stock amplifies market moves; below one means it dampens them.

The security market line

The CAPM's prediction is a straight line: expected excess return equals beta times the market risk premium. Risk that can be diversified away earns no premium; only the non-diversifiable, market-related part of risk is priced. Plotting average returns against betas across stocks gives an empirical version of this line.

Estimation and caveats

Beta is estimated over a rolling window, and the choice of window length and return frequency matters. The CAPM is also the model the data most famously pushes back on — the empirical relation between beta and average return is much flatter than theory predicts — which is exactly what motivates the multi-factor models in later chapters.
